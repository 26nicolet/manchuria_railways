# Building the 1940 Manchuria Railway Tracker: Process and Design Decisions

## 1. Extracting Timetable Data from Historical Documents

The project began with a 1940 Japanese railway timetable published in Kyūjitai (traditional) characters — a PDF of scanned pages containing dense tabular schedules, hotel directories, and maps. Extracting structured data from these images required an OCR pipeline built around Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite model. Each page was rasterized at 300 DPI using PyMuPDF, then sent to the LLM with a carefully crafted system prompt instructing it to act as an expert historian of pre-WWII Japanese railways. The prompt had to explicitly instruct the model to preserve Kyūjitai characters rather than normalizing them to modern Shinjitai, since the station names in the timetable (e.g., 奉天, 新京, 京城) are historically specific and differ from their modern equivalents.

The major challenge was reliability. The model sometimes entered infinite loops when encountering hotel directory pages mixed with timetables, which required adding deduplication instructions to the prompt. Rate limiting from the API necessitated a retry-with-backoff strategy. Pages were processed in batches of 2–3, and failures were logged and retried separately. The output — roughly 24,000 timetable entries across 39 JSON files — preserved the original structure of train numbers, station names, arrival/departure times, and stop types.

## 2. Filtering and Preparing the OSM Railway Network

The OpenStreetMap railway dataset for China is enormous (over 100 MB of GeoJSON), far more than needed for the Manchuria-focused project. A spatial pre-filtering script (`filter_osm.py`) used a streaming line-by-line parser to extract only features with at least one coordinate falling within a generous bounding box (latitude 36°–56°, longitude 116°–136°), covering the region from Beijing to Vladivostok. This approach avoided loading the entire file into memory while still capturing long-distance lines that crossed the boundaries.

The filtered output was further refined using an interactive selection tool (`select_routes.html`) built with MapLibre GL. This tool offered rectangle selection, freeform lasso selection (using ray-casting point-in-polygon tests), and individual click toggling — allowing manual curation of which railway lines to include. The design decision to build a visual selection tool rather than relying purely on automated filtering proved essential: automated approaches couldn't distinguish between active railway lines, abandoned tracks, subway lines, and industrial spurs that all appear in OSM data. The curated output (`selected_railways.geojson`, ~96,000 features) formed the basis of the railway graph.

## 3. Building the Data Pipeline

The core pipeline (`build_data.py`) transforms raw timetable entries and OSM geometry into web-ready JSON through eight stages. Station coordinates were geocoded separately and stored in `stations_web.json`, then filtered to a Manchuria bounding box (latitude 38°–54°, longitude 118°–135°) with additional corridor bounding boxes extending to Beijing-Tianjin (latitude 38.5°–41.5°, longitude 115.5°–120.5°) and the Korean peninsula (latitude 37.0°–40.5°, longitude 124°–130°). Key cities like Beijing, Tianjin, and Seoul were force-included regardless of bounding box membership, since the historical railway network connected these cities to the Manchurian core.

Route construction from raw timetable entries required solving a deduplication problem: different railway networks in the source data shared the same train numbers, producing nonsensical routes that jumped between distant regions. The solution was a two-pass splitting strategy. First, temporal splitting broke routes at gaps exceeding 12 hours or negative jumps exceeding 2 hours. Second, geographic coherence splitting calculated the implied speed between consecutive stops using the Haversine formula and split the route whenever speed exceeded 150 km/h — an impossibility for 1940s steam locomotives. This MAX_SPEED_KMH parameter was the key design decision that separated genuinely connected routes from coincidental train number collisions.

## 4. Snapping Routes to Railway Geometry

The most algorithmically complex step was routing trains along actual railway lines rather than straight lines between stations. The OSM features were assembled into a NetworkX graph with coordinates as nodes and edge weights proportional to Euclidean distance. A proximity filter (NEAR_THRESH = 0.8°, approximately 80 km) kept only OSM features with at least one sampled point within range of a known station, using a scipy cKDTree for efficient spatial queries. This threshold was initially set to 0.3° (~30 km) but had to be increased after discovering that long-haul routes like the Ha-Jia line (哈佳线, Harbin to Jiamusi, ~500 km) had stretches exceeding 120 km between stations, causing the graph to sever in the middle.

Each station was snapped to its nearest graph node within a 25 km threshold. However, the resulting graph often contained dozens of disconnected components — the Manchuria railways, synthetic North Korea segments, and South Korean OSM data existed as separate islands. A bridge_components() function iteratively connected nearby components by sampling boundary nodes (degree-1 endpoints plus uniform samples for large components), building a KDTree over them, and adding bridge edges for cross-component pairs within 0.05° (~5 km). This reduced the component count from over 80 to under 20 across multiple iterations.

For each consecutive station pair on a train route, the A* algorithm found the shortest path through the railway graph, with Euclidean distance as the heuristic. Connected component IDs were pre-computed so that unreachable pairs could be detected instantly rather than triggering expensive failed searches. A path cache stored computed routes to avoid redundant work for popular corridors. When no rail path existed, the system fell back to a straight line marked with `geom_type: 'straight'` — a visual signal of incomplete coverage. The final build achieved roughly 2,880 rail-snapped route segments against 2,050 straight-line fallbacks.

## 5. Handling the North Korea Data Gap

North Korean railways are almost entirely absent from OpenStreetMap. Since the historical network included major lines connecting Manchuria to Korea — the Gyeongui Main Line from Andong (安東) through Sinuiju and Pyongyang to Seoul, plus several branch lines — this gap had to be filled. A script (`add_nk_railways.py`) manually defined 45 synthetic LineString segments from historical map coordinates for six railway lines. Each route was broken into individual two-point segments using a segmentize() function, improving graph connectivity during the bridging phase by ensuring each fragment could independently match with nearby components.

South Korean OSM data was separately downloaded and merged into the main GeoJSON. The Korea corridor bounding box was deliberately narrowed to latitude 37.0° (roughly the Seoul area) to exclude southern stations like Busan and Daegu that had no corresponding timetable data. This was a pragmatic design decision: showing stations with no trains running would confuse users without adding historical value.

## 6. Creating the Interactive Webpage

The visualization was built as a single-page application using Leaflet.js with CARTO's light_all basemap tiles — chosen over satellite or dark basemaps because the light gray provides clean contrast against the red railway lines without competing for visual attention. The UI centers on a yellow (#F5C518) topbar featuring a Manchukuo flag SVG, reflecting the historical context of the 1940 timetable.

The train animation system pre-computes cumulative distances along each route's geometry at page load, enabling smooth position interpolation during playback. When a train is active (between its departure and arrival times), the system calculates its fractional progress between stops, then uses binary search on the pre-computed distance array to find the exact interpolated position along curves. Train dots scale with zoom level (3–7 pixels) to remain visible at overview scales without cluttering at detail scales. Playback supports 1x, 2x, and 4x speeds with a scrubber for manual time navigation.

Bilingual station support maps 187 Chinese names to 1940s-era English romanizations (奉天 → Mukden, 新京 → Hsinking, 京城 → Keijo) and also recognizes modern English names (Shenyang, Changchun, Seoul). All search, autocomplete, tooltips, and panels display both Chinese and English. The design decision to use historical romanizations rather than modern pinyin reflects the project's commitment to representing the world as a 1940s traveler would have encountered it. A lodging panel provides historical hotel data for six major cities, including pricing in 1940s Yen, drawn directly from the source timetable's hotel directory pages.

## 7. Building the Trip Planner

The trip planner implements a time-expanded Dijkstra search that finds the earliest-arriving itinerary between any two stations, including transfers. The algorithm maintains a priority queue of states, each representing a traveler at a specific station and time with an accumulated itinerary. At each step, it finds all train departures from the current station after a minimum transfer time of 10 minutes, then propagates forward to every subsequent stop on each departing train.

The key design challenges were preventing combinatorial explosion and avoiding duplicate results. A `best` map tracks the earliest known arrival at each station, pruning states that can't improve on existing solutions. Transfers are capped at 4 to prevent pathological itineraries. A route deduplication set prevents boarding the same train multiple times from a single state. The search terminates after finding 3 distinct itineraries or exhausting 50,000 iterations. Results display each leg with departure/arrival times, train numbers, and explicit wait times at transfers — formatted as hours and minutes for human readability.

## 8. Fixing Station Coordinates

Geocoded station coordinates from the source database frequently placed stations kilometers away from their actual railway locations — sometimes in the wrong town entirely. Linkou (林口), for example, was positioned roughly 20 km from the real Linkou station in Heilongjiang, causing the entire Lin-Mi line (林密线) to render as a straight line rather than snapping to rail geometry. Each misplaced station was identified by visual inspection on the map (comparing the blue station dot against the basemap's labeled town locations) and corrected by looking up the railway station coordinates on OpenStreetMap's Nominatim geocoder.

Over two rounds of fixes, 19 stations had their coordinates corrected (including major nodes like Qiqihar, Gongzhuling, and Kaiyuan), and 11 defunct stations were removed entirely — stations where the historical rail line no longer exists in OSM or where no timetable data justified their inclusion. Some stations required special handling: 千山 (Chienshan) had been renamed to Anshan and needed both a coordinate fix and a conceptual reclassification. 羅津 (Rajin) was removed because it lies in North Korea with no meaningful rail connectivity in the current dataset. Each fix required a full pipeline rebuild (roughly 5 minutes) to re-snap all affected routes, since a single station's coordinate change can cascade through dozens of train routes that pass through it.
